MedMulti

A multi-agent diagnostic support system for private hospitals. Runs entirely on local infrastructure with open-source models. Thinks by structured disagreement between specialist agents, learns continuously from every doctor's feedback.

Local Deployment Multi-Agent Continuous Learning
Part 1

System Design

A single LLM call is not enough for medical diagnosis. The system emulates a team of doctors conferring on a difficult case — generating, challenging, and refining the diagnosis across multiple rounds before producing a report. All inference runs locally on open-source models; only the public knowledge base queries cross the network boundary.

Part 2

The Thinking Loop — Multi-Agent Deliberation

The core insight: a model cannot see its own blind spots. Four specialist agents engage in structured rounds of proposal, challenge, evidence audit, and synthesis. Disagreements are surfaced, not hidden.

🩺
Primary Diagnostician
Generates and defends
Produces the initial differential diagnosis with full reasoning chain and explicit assumptions. Iterates based on peer challenges in subsequent rounds.
⚔️
Devil's Advocate
Challenges constructively
Must find at least 2 challenges. Identifies missing evidence, alternative explanations, and cognitive biases (anchoring, confirmation, availability).
📚
Evidence Reviewer
Cross-references claims
Audits every diagnostic claim against both knowledge bases. Labels each claim: supported, partially supported, or unsupported. Retrieves relevant guidelines and case studies.
📋
Synthesizer
Integrates and reports
Combines all perspectives into a consensus report. Preserves unresolved disagreements prominently. Produces the structured final output.

Deliberation Protocol

R1
Generate — Primary Diagnostician
Produces differential diagnosis with full reasoning chain and explicitly states every assumption it is making about the patient, the symptoms, and the evidence.
Primary Diagnostician
R2
Challenge & Audit — Devil's Advocate + Evidence Reviewer
Devil's Advocate identifies weaknesses, alternative hypotheses, and potential biases. Evidence Reviewer cross-references every claim against both KBs and assigns support levels. Both run in parallel.
Devil's AdvocateEvidence Reviewer
R3
Revise — Primary Diagnostician
Addresses every challenge. Refines diagnosis, adjusts confidence, incorporates evidence audit. If a challenge is valid, the diagnosis changes. If not, explains why the challenge does not apply.
Primary Diagnostician
R4
Synthesize — Synthesizer
Integrates all perspectives into the final report. Preserves disagreements that could not be resolved. Outputs the structured diagnosis report ready for doctor review.
Synthesizer
↻ Divergence Handling
Convergence → early exit. If the Devil's Advocate and Primary Diagnostician reach agreement (scored by an agreement metric), the loop exits after R3 and skips directly to synthesis.
Divergence → extra rounds. If disagreement exceeds threshold, additional R2–R3 cycles are triggered, each with deeper evidence retrieval and sharper challenge framing. The system will not produce a report until either convergence or a maximum round limit (6) is hit.
Part 3

Diagnosis Report Output

The report is not a single answer — it is a structured document that shows the reasoning, the evidence, the assumptions, and most importantly, what the system is unsure about. This is what makes it safe for clinical use.

Primary Diagnosis
Condition: Community-acquired pneumonia (right lower lobe)
Confidence: 0.82 — supported by fever + cough + right basilar crackles + opacity on CXR
Supporting evidence: 4 findings matched · 2 guideline references · 3 similar cases in hospital history
Missing evidence: Sputum culture pending; atypical pathogen panel not yet ordered
Differential Diagnosis
1. Pulmonary embolism — confidence 0.21
  Why ruled out: No risk factors (no recent surgery, no DVT signs, Wells score = 2). D-dimer would resolve.
2. Bronchitis (acute) — confidence 0.15
  Why ruled out: CXR shows focal consolidation inconsistent with bronchitis alone.
Unresolved Disagreements
Disagreement: Devil's Advocate argues the opacity could be an early mass, not infection.
Primary Diagnostician position: Clinical picture (acute fever, productive cough) strongly favors infection.
Devil's Advocate position: Patient is a 30-pack-year smoker; malignancy must be in differential.
Recommendation: Repeat CXR in 6 weeks after treatment. If opacity persists, order CT chest.
Recommended Next Steps
Tests: CBC with diff · Sputum culture + gram stain · Blood cultures ×2 · D-dimer (per disagreement)
Treatment: Start empiric antibiotics per hospital CAP protocol (ceftriaxone + azithromycin)
Referrals: None required at this stage
Urgency: Urgent — admit for IV antibiotics
Assumptions Made
1. Patient has no antibiotic allergies (not documented in chart)
2. The CXR opacity is acute, not chronic (no prior imaging for comparison)
3. Patient can tolerate oral intake for antibiotic step-down
Sources Cited
IDSA/ATS 2019 CAP Guidelines · Hospital CAP Protocol v3.2 · 3 similar cases from internal KB (anonymized: #1247, #1301, #1312)

The Unresolved Disagreements and Assumptions Made sections are the most important safety features. They tell the doctor: "Here is what we are unsure about, and here is what we assumed — please verify."

Part 4

Continuous Learning — Feedback as Training Data

Every doctor interaction is a learning signal. The system treats feedback not as an annotation but as training data. It flows into three improvement pathways simultaneously.

Four Feedback Actions

✓ Confirm
Diagnosis is correct. Stored as a positive example. Increases the case weight for similar presentations in future retrievals.
✗ Correct
Diagnosis is wrong. Doctor provides the correct diagnosis + reasoning. Creates a DPO preference pair: doctor's answer > system's answer.
↻ Refine
Diagnosis is correct but reasoning path was suboptimal. Doctor provides the better reasoning chain. Also creates a DPO pair.
+ Add Evidence
Doctor flags evidence the system missed. That evidence source is indexed and added to the KB for future retrievals.

Three Improvement Pathways

1
Preference Pairs → DPO Model Alignment
Corrections and refinements generate pairwise preferences: doctor's answer > system's answer. Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) is applied periodically (weekly/monthly) to align the model with accumulated clinical judgment. DPO is chosen over RLHF because it is simpler, more stable, and works directly with explicit pairwise preferences without needing a separate reward model.
2
KB Enrichment
Every corrected case is anonymized and added to the Internal KB case history — the single most valuable data source for future diagnoses. Doctor-provided reasoning is stored as expert annotation on the case. Missing evidence flagged by doctors is indexed and linked.
3
Protocol Evolution Signals
When doctors consistently override the system for a specific condition, the pattern is flagged for protocol review. This closes the loop between the AI system and the hospital's own evolving clinical practice.

Why DPO (Not RLHF)

PropertyRLHFDPO
Requires reward modelYes — separate model to trainNo — optimizes preferences directly
Training stabilityFragile; needs careful reward hacking mitigationStable; simple cross-entropy-like loss

Fit for small-batch updatesPoor — reward model needs large dataGood — works with accumulated batches

Safety Guardrails

GuardrailMechanism
Low confidence escalationIf confidence < 0.5, report carries an explicit warning banner
Unresolved disagreementAgents that cannot converge show disagreement prominently at the top of the report
Assumption surfacingEvery assumption is listed explicitly — nothing is silently assumed
Doctor as deciderSystem suggests; doctor decides. No treatment recommendation is actionable without review
Emergency detectionKeywords (chest pain + radiating, sudden severe headache, etc.) flag report as URGENT
Out-of-distribution detectionIf the case is far from any KB match, the system states uncertainty instead of guessing
